How we’re building this · consumer UX direction

Two journeys the wallet owns:
Add and Prove.

Getting a document in, and sharing the minimum to prove something. A phone’s chooser may decide which wallet answers a website — but everything that defines trust happens here, in the wallet.

The platform may own

  • Which wallet or document is offered to a website (the chooser sheet).

The wallet always owns

  • Authenticating the verifier & its registered purpose
  • Consent, and sharing the minimum
  • Your approval (Face ID / passcode) & building the response
  • Recovery, and the durable home for your documents

Both iOS and Android let our wallet participate as a provider — Apple gives the selected provider its own authorization UI, and the EU framework keeps consent and relying-party authentication with the wallet, not the browser or OS. So consent is a flagship journey, not an afterthought.

Accessibility is a design constraint, not a pass at the end

Use the “Aa Large text” toggle at the top to see every screen scale. The rules below are baked into these frames.

What we hold to

  • Large, adjustable type — the whole prototype honours a large-text setting (toggle above); nothing is pinned to a tiny size.
  • 44 pt minimum controls, full-width primary buttons, generous spacing between tap targets.
  • High contrast — dark text on light (and the reverse in dark mode); no low-contrast grey for anything a person must read or do.
  • No card-within-card — flat grouped rows and dividers, so structure is scannable, not nested boxes.
  • Critical instructions stay on screen — never below the fold; the primary action is always reachable without scrolling.
  • Plain verbs — Add, Check, Tap, Confirm, Ready; no “PID”, no protocol words, no jargon.
  • Reduced motion respected; the tap animation is decorative only, with a text status beside it.

VoiceOver & testing

  • Every screen has a reading order and spoken labels (expand “VoiceOver order & labels” under each frame).
  • Screens announce as a heading first, then context, then actions — so a listener knows where they are before what to do.
  • Live regions narrate progress and interruptions (“Reading your card… connection lost, reposition”).
  • Clickable prototype: this page is intended for moderated sessions — sit beside a participant, let them talk through “Add your National ID” and “Prove your age”, and watch where hesitation happens (the PIN question and the tap are the moments to study).
  • No time claims shown to users until measured with the real Ausweis SDK, network, issuer, and PIN-recovery paths.
